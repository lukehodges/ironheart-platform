# Acceler8 Demo Day Strategy — Full Spec

**Date:** 2026-03-20
**Event:** Acceler8 Demo Day, University of Bath
**When:** ~1 week from now
**Format:** 7-minute pitch + 5-minute Q&A + networking
**Audience:** University executives — heads of student union, School of Management, funding departments, admin leadership, plus local businesses and invited guests
**Goal:** Walk out with 3-5 meetings booked with university department heads and local business leaders for paid discovery engagements

---

## Part 1: The Core Insight

Bath's own Digital, Data and Technology team (DDaT — 145 staff) is currently running 5 major transformation projects simultaneously: Curriculum Transformation, Data & Insights Hub, Agresso cloud migration, research management, and CRM enhancement. Their own publications admit data is siloed across SAMIS, Moodle, XPlan, Pure, Agresso, Archibus, and Syllabus+ with no easy aggregation.

This means every department that needs a custom tool, a workflow automated, or systems connected is sitting in a queue behind enterprise-scale programmes. When they go external, they get quoted enterprise prices — because the vendors they know are enterprise vendors.

That's the gap. Ironheart sits in the space between "wait 18 months for internal IT" and "pay a Big Four consultancy £200k for a PDF." Fast, focused, affordable delivery at the department level — without adding to DDaT's backlog.

The pitch doesn't name DDaT or criticise Bath's IT team. It describes the pattern so clearly that every executive in the room recognises their own situation.

## Part 2: Pitch Deck — Slide by Slide

### Slide 1 — The Hook (45 seconds)

**On screen:** Black slide. White text.

> "Every large organisation has the same problem.
> Seven systems. None of them talk to each other.
> And the team that could fix it is booked out for the next two years."

**What you say:**

"Every university, every council, every large organisation I've worked with has the same pattern. Your data lives in seven different systems. Your staff spend hours every week copying information from one place to another. And the internal team that could fix it — they're brilliant, but they're running five transformation projects already. They're not going to build your department a custom dashboard any time soon. That's not a criticism. That's just how large organisations work. And it's exactly where I come in."

**Why this works:** Every executive in that room manages a department that has this exact problem. You're not attacking Bath's IT — you're describing a universal reality they all live with. They'll be nodding.

---

### Slide 2 — The Cost of Doing Nothing (45 seconds)

**On screen:** Three bullets, revealed one at a time:

- Staff spending 10+ hours a week on tasks a system should handle
- Paying for enterprise software that does 5% of what you bought it for
- "Digital transformation" projects that take 18 months and change nothing

**What you say:**

"Here's what that pattern actually costs. Your admin staff are buried in manual work — re-entering data, chasing emails, updating spreadsheets that should update themselves. You're paying thousands a year for software licenses nobody fully uses. And when someone finally commissions a transformation project, it takes 18 months, costs six figures, and the people on the ground say nothing actually changed. The people in this room have lived this. You know exactly what I'm describing."

**Why this works:** You're not telling them they have a problem. You're describing their Tuesday. The "10+ hours a week" figure is specific enough to make them do the mental calculation for their own team.

### Slide 3 — What We Do (30 seconds)

**On screen:**

> **Ironheart — Operational AI Consultancy**
>
> We find the 20% of your operations causing 80% of the pain — and build systems that fix it permanently.
>
> **Audit. Build. Operate.**

**What you say:**

"Ironheart is an AI consultancy. We don't hand you a report and walk away. We come in, audit your day-to-day operations — not your IT infrastructure, your actual processes — identify the highest-impact problems, and then we build the systems that solve them. And we stay accountable until it's working."

**Why this works:** "Audit. Build. Operate." is a three-word framework they can remember and repeat. It differentiates you from consultancies (who stop at "Audit") and dev shops (who skip straight to "Build"). The word "permanently" signals you're not selling maintenance contracts.

Pause.

"Four clients. Four industries. Four completely different problems. One approach."

**Why this works:** Four delivered projects across four industries is remarkable credibility. The regulated industry mention (healthcare, environmental compliance) signals you can handle the governance and data sensitivity that universities care about. These aren't student projects — they're production systems with paying clients.

### Slide 5 — The Platform Advantage (60 seconds)

**On screen:** Two side-by-side screenshots — one showing the platform configured as a healthcare booking system, one showing it configured as a brokerage/compliance system. Different dashboards, different data, same UI framework. Below them:

> "One platform. Already built for four industries.
> Adapting it to yours takes weeks, not months."

**What you say:**

"Behind all of this is something that makes us fundamentally different from a traditional consultancy or dev shop. We've built an operational platform that already handles bookings, customer management, workflows, compliance tracking, forms, payments, and AI — across multiple industries. When we take on a new client, we're not starting from scratch. We're configuring what already exists and building the 20% that's specific to you. That's why we can deliver in weeks what other firms quote months for. And it's why our prices are a fraction of what you'd expect."

Then, if the AI chat is working at all — show a 20-second clip or live demo:

"And this is the part that changes everything. You can talk to the system in plain English. 'How many bookings do we have this week?' 'Show me all overdue compliance items.' 'Cancel tomorrow's 2pm and rebook for Thursday.' It understands your operations because it's connected to every part of the platform."

**Why this works:** University executives don't need to understand the architecture. They need to understand two things: (1) this already exists, so it's fast and cheap to deploy, and (2) the AI means their staff can interact with it naturally instead of learning another complex system. The side-by-side screenshots prove versatility without explaining module manifests.

## Part 3: Q&A Strategy (5 Minutes)

### Framework

Every answer follows the same structure:
1. Short, confident answer (15-20 seconds max)
2. Bridge to a conversation: "That varies by organisation — I'd love to dig into your specific situation afterwards."

The Q&A is not a defence of your business model. It's 5 minutes of additional opportunities to say "let's chat after."

### Likely Questions and Answers

**"How big is your team?"**
"Ironheart operates lean by design. I lead every engagement personally — clients get the senior person, not a junior analyst learning on their account. For specialist work, I bring in domain experts from a vetted network. It means you're paying for output, not overhead. We're expanding our delivery team later this year."

**"What does an engagement typically cost?"**
"It depends entirely on scope, but a typical first project runs between five and twenty-five thousand pounds. That's a fraction of what a traditional consultancy charges because we're building on a platform that already exists — we're not starting from zero every time. The best way to figure out what makes sense for you is a short scoping call."

**"How is this different from what our IT team does?"**
"Your IT team should be focused on the big-picture transformation work — data governance, platform migrations, security. That's exactly what a central technology team should do. What I handle is the department-level operational tools that sit in the queue behind those strategic programmes. I'm not replacing your IT team — I'm the fast lane for the projects they can't get to yet."

**"What about data security and governance?"**
"Everything we build runs on enterprise-grade infrastructure with full tenant isolation — each department's data is completely separated. We build to WCAG accessibility standards, we support SSO integration, and we can work within your existing data governance policies. We've already built systems for regulated industries — healthcare and environmental compliance — where data governance is non-negotiable."

**"You're a student here, aren't you?"**
"I studied Computer Science here — that's actually where I first saw how much operational inefficiency runs beneath the surface of large institutions. Since then, Ironheart has delivered production systems across four industries with paying clients who rely on them every day."

Then immediately pivot to a client result. The "student" label dissolves the moment you talk about real commercial work.

**"Can you give a specific example of what you'd build for a university department?"**
"Absolutely. Take placements — one of the biggest operational headaches for any faculty. Right now, matching students to employers, tracking applications, chasing insurance forms, managing compliance checks — that's probably a spreadsheet and a lot of emails. I could deploy a system where students apply through a portal, employers post positions, matching happens automatically based on criteria, compliance documents are tracked with deadline alerts, and the placements team has a dashboard showing exactly where every student is in the process. And they can ask the system in plain English: 'Which students haven't submitted their DBS check?' That's the kind of thing I build."

**"What sectors do you focus on?"**
"Any organisation with complex operational workflows — especially traditional industries and institutions that haven't had access to modern technology at an affordable price. Healthcare, professional services, environmental, events, and increasingly education. The platform is industry-agnostic — it's the operational patterns that transfer."

## Part 4: Room-Working Playbook

### Before Demo Day

**Intelligence gathering (do this immediately):**
- Ask the Acceler8 organisers for the guest list or at least which departments/roles are represented
- LinkedIn-search every name you get. Note their role and any digital transformation mentions
- For each target, write one sentence about what problem you could solve for them

**Pre-event outreach (3-5 people max):**
Send short LinkedIn messages to the highest-value attendees:

> "Hi [Name], I saw you're attending the Acceler8 demo day. I'm presenting Ironheart — we build AI-powered operational systems for organisations that want to cut admin time without hiring a full tech team. Would love to hear about what [their department] is working on. See you there."

Goal: make your name familiar so when you pitch, they think "that's the person who messaged me."

**Target prioritisation:**

| Tier | Who | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | School of Management, Student Union leadership, Research Office, Admissions | Budget + pain + decision-making authority |
| 2 | Faculty operations managers, HR Services, Estates/Facilities | High operational complexity, likely drowning in manual processes |
| 3 | Local businesses, other founders, friends | Referral value, potential future clients |

Spend 80% of networking time on Tier 1 and 2.

### During Q&A

Steer every answer toward "let's chat after this." Never let a single questioner dominate — after one exchange: "Great question — let's dig into that afterwards. Who else?"

### Post-Pitch Networking (The Critical 30-60 Minutes)

**First 5 minutes after you leave the stage:** Do not go to the drinks table. Do not check your phone. Stand near the front, slightly to the side. Anyone who approaches in the first 5 minutes is a warm lead.

**The 3-minute conversation framework:**

1. **Ask about them (60 sec):** "What's your role at the university?" / "What kind of operational challenges does your team deal with?" — Listen for: "manual," "spreadsheet," "backlog," "we've been meaning to," "we tried but."
2. **Connect their pain to your work (30 sec):** "That's exactly what we fixed for [similar client]. We took a [X]-hour process down to [Y] minutes."
3. **Propose next step (30 sec):** "Could we book a twenty-minute call next week? I'll come with some ideas specific to your situation."
4. **Book it on the spot.** Pull out your phone. Open your calendar. Don't say "I'll send you something." Book it there.
5. **Exchange details and move on (30 sec).** Give them your card. Get their email. "Brilliant — I'll send you a note tomorrow." Move to the next person.

Three minutes per person. You want breadth, not depth — depth comes in the follow-up meetings.

### Follow-Up (Within 24 Hours)

**Same evening:** Write down every name, conversation, pain point mentioned, and meeting booked before you sleep.

**Next morning (before 10am):** Send a personalised email to every person you spoke with:

> Subject: Good to meet you at Acceler8, [First Name]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Really enjoyed our conversation about [specific thing they mentioned].
>
> As I mentioned, we've helped organisations tackle exactly that kind of [problem area] — [one-line result from a relevant case study].
>
> Would you be open to a 20-minute call this week to explore what this could look like for [their department]? Here are a few times: [3 specific slots].
>
> Best,
> Luke Hodges
> Ironheart | [phone] | theironheart.org

Rules: reference their specific problem, propose specific times, under 100 words, no attachments.

**LinkedIn:** Connect with everyone you met within 48 hours with a personalised note.

## Part 7: Procurement Reality (For Your Follow-Up Meetings)

University of Bath procurement thresholds:

| Spend | Process |
|---|---|
| Under £10,000 | Department can purchase directly — no formal process |
| £10,000 - £25,000 | Bid Evaluation Document required — lightweight |
| £25,000 - £40,000 | BED + procurement staff approval |
| Over £40,000 | Formal competitive tender |

**Strategy:** First engagement under £10k. A department head can approve this from the room.

**Timing tailwind:** Bath's financial year ends 31 July. Pitching in late March means departments are in Q4 — often with unspent budget they need to use or lose. This works in your favour.

**Pricing ladder:**
1. **Paid diagnostic / audit** — £5k-£8k (under threshold, virtually no process)
2. **MVP / prototype build** — £15k-£22k (BED form only)
3. **Full build + ongoing** — £30k-£50k (by this point you have an internal champion)

Position the free 20-minute call as the gateway to the paid audit. The paid audit is the gateway to the build.

## Part 9: What Not To Do

- **Do not use the £150k logos line directly.** It will embarrass people in the room and close doors. Instead, describe the pattern: "Most organisations I talk to have spent six figures on projects that should have cost a fraction."
- **Do not mention module manifests, tRPC, vertical configs, or any technical architecture.** The platform is your advantage — they don't need to see the engine.
- **Do not pitch for funding.** You're not seeking investment. If asked, say: "We're revenue-funded. Every engagement pays for itself."
- **Do not include a TAM/SAM/SOM slide, revenue projections, competitive landscape matrix, or roadmap.** This is not a VC pitch.
- **Do not include a "team" slide.** Don't draw attention to team size. If asked, use the "lean by design" framing.
- **Do not read from slides.** Every slide has minimal text. You are the presentation.
- **Do not spend networking time with other founders or friends.** Catch up with them later. Your limited time goes to Tier 1 targets.
- **Do not say "I know I'm young" or apologise for your background.** Ever.
- **Do not criticise Bath's IT team.** Position as complementary: "I handle the department-level tools that sit in the queue behind strategic programmes."

## Part 10: Solo Founder Credibility

**Language rules:**
- Use "I" for personal conviction: "I started Ironheart because..."
- Use "we" for the operation: "We scope every project around measurable ROI"
- Never say "my company." Say "Ironheart" as a proper noun — it sounds like an entity

**If team size comes up:**
"Ironheart operates lean by design. Clients get the senior person — me — not a junior analyst learning on their account. For specialist delivery, I work with a network of vetted engineers. It means you're not paying for overhead. We're expanding the team later this year."

**Credibility signals:**
1. Specific numbers beat vague claims: "Reduced a 4-hour process to 12 minutes" > "we improve efficiency"
2. Named industries, even without naming clients: "healthcare, environmental compliance, professional services"
3. The working platform itself — a live system proves you can build
4. Speed: "We took a client from scoping to live system in three weeks"

**Deck design cues:**
- Dark backgrounds, white text, one accent colour. No templates that look like student presentations
- No stock photos. Use real screenshots, simple diagrams, or just text on dark
- One sans-serif font, two weights (regular + bold)
- Ironheart logo on every slide in a fixed position
- No "About the Team" slide
